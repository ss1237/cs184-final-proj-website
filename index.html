Abstract

We present Shapeshift which is a system for interpolating between two 3D triangle meshes with differing topologies. Traditional morphing techniques often assume topological consistency between source and target meshes, limiting their applicability in real-world shape transformations. Shapeshift addresses this by leveraging the halfedge data structure that we learnt and implemented in HW2 to enable topology-aware interpolation. Our method first upsamples the source mesh, establishing vertex correspondences through geometric matching and projection onto the target mesh. Residual vertices are iteratively removed to ensure topological alignment. Once aligned, we perform linear vertex interpolation to generate smooth shape transitions. We additionally explore extensions such as shading and texture interpolation to enhance visual continuity.

Technical Approach

Vertex Pairing with the Hungarian Algorithm

The foundation of our morphing system is a vertex matching stage. We treat the problem of finding the mapping between the source and target mesh vertices as an assignment problem, where we minimize the global cost of pairing. The Hungarian Algorithm (Kuhn, 1955) makes a bijective mapping between vertices in the upsampled source mesh and the target mesh. This enables us to define a smooth one-to-one correspondence between shapes, even if their original vertex counts differ.


Mesh Upsampling, Scaling and Centroid Normalization

However, before we do the vertex mapping, how can we ensure that there is at least one vertex in the target mesh for each vertex in the source mesh? How can we actually do a mapping between them both? First, we have to set up the problem so that we can. This required some experimentation:

To facilitate meaningful correspondences, we first upsample the source mesh using Loop subdivision until it contains at least twice as many vertices as the target mesh. This guarantees sufficient resolution to match the more compact target mesh features. Then, we normalize both meshes by translating them to place their centroids at the origin, removing biases introduced by file-specific coordinate systems.

After normalization, we uniformly scale the source mesh until it completely encloses the target mesh. This empirical step ensures that vertices in the source mesh envelop all areas of the target shape, improving the likelihood that each region of the target mesh has a corresponding point nearby in the source mesh — particularly important for matching meshes with varying size or geometric scope.


Projection and Vertex Remapping

After matching the two meshes spatially, some vertices in the source mesh may remain unmatched, particularly those added during the upsampling step. Rather than discard them or introduce artifacts, we project these unmatched vertices onto the surface of the target mesh using closest-point projection. This yields a plausible correspondence without requiring global surface parameterization.

Once projected, these vertices are interpolated toward their nearest points on the target mesh surface, allowing us to preserve the full detail of the subdivided source while ensuring each point has a meaningful destination. This projection technique serves as a practical and flexible alternative to complex remeshing pipelines such as those in MAPS.


Vertex Interpolation and Temporal Morphing

With all source vertices now assigned to final positions — either through Hungarian matching or projection — we use linear interpolation to morph between the original and target vertex positions. Over a user-defined number of frames, each vertex follows a straight-line path from its origin to destination.

This simple interpolation method works well due to the prior normalization, scaling, and matching steps, which ensure the morph appears globally smooth. It also avoids the numerical instability or complexity of solving partial differential equations, while still yielding perceptually pleasing transitions.


Comparison to MAPS

Our approach differs significantly from MAPS (Multiresolution Adaptive Parameterization of Surfaces) [Lee et al., 1998], which requires manually creating a shared base domain between two meshes and constructing hierarchical parameterizations. However, our method was heavily inspired from MAPS. Instead of manually creating a shared base domain for the two given meshes, our method of upsampling and Hungarian matching creates a pseudo-base domain that can be used as a connection between the two meshes. Afterwards, we discard the hierarchical parameterizations in favor of a simple linear interpolation in favor of ease of implementation and greater flexibility. This meant that we were only able to work with simpler, genus-0 meshes, which was acceptable for our project goals.


Problems & Lessons Learned

As seen in our midpoint presentation, our initial implementation used simple Hungarian matching, which we believed would suffice for all topologically equivalent meshes. However, as we experimented with more complex examples, we realized that a more sophisticated approach was necessary. This led us to explore the MAPS paper, from which we adopted the idea of a shared base domain. Since constructing a unique shared base domain for any two meshes would be infeasible, we instead chose to upsample our initial mesh and apply the Hungarian algorithm – effectively treating the final mesh as the base domain, with the upsampled initial mesh having one-to-one mappings to it.

Another point of improvisation arose when assigning the unmapped source vertices after the Hungarian matching. In traditional MAPS, it is often necessary to maintain the subdivision hierarchy to reverse it and project unmatched vertices onto the base domain. After implementing vertex deletion and attempting this approach, we found it too complex for our scope – leading us to adopt linear interpolation for the final morphing step.
